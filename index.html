⚠ OSMOTIC DEMYELINATION SYNDROME (ODS) RISK
Maximum correction rate: <8 mEq/L per 24h (absolute ceiling 10 mEq/L/24h, 18 mEq/L/48h). Risk highest in chronic hyponatremia, alcoholism, malnutrition, hypokalemia, liver disease. Overcorrection: D5W ± DDAVP to re-lower. Recheck Na q4–6h.

Preferred for hypovolemic hyponatremia. Avoid in SIADH/euvolemic states.
NS 0.9% not appropriate. Serum Na ≥154 mEq/L means NS provides no net Na rise. Use NaCl 3% instead.
Classify first: hypovolemic vs euvolemic (SIADH) vs hypervolemic (CHF, cirrhosis) — guides fluid choice.
Severe symptomatic (seizure, coma): bolus NaCl 3% 100–150 mL × 1–3, target ΔNa 4–6 mEq/L acutely, then stop.
Recheck Na q2–4h initially; q6h once stable. Track urine output.

Oral preferred when K ≥3.0, asymptomatic, intact GI absorption. Max 40 mEq/dose; split across day. Take with food.
Always check Mg: hypomagnesemia causes refractory hypokalemia. Replace MgSO₄ in parallel.
Renal function: reduce dose if eGFR <30 or oliguric — risk of hyperkalemia.
Diluent: use NS over D5W (dextrose → insulin → intracellular K shift, paradoxical drop).
Recheck K 2–4h after each replacement; sooner if ECG changes or rate >10 mEq/h.
